In analog circuits, the signal is represented by the magnitude of the voltage.

In digital circuits, only two states of a voltage are distinguished: it can have a high level, which in positive logic corresponds to the logical state 1, or a low level, which is associated with the state 0.

One consequence of this is that transistors in digital circuits are operated only at two extreme operating points: conducting or cut off.

The link between the analog and the digital world is provided by the comparator. A comparator compares two input voltages in magnitude and produces a binary output signal that is either in the state

- High ≙ 1
- Low ≙ 0

This assignment is called _positive logic_.

The actual values of the corresponding input and output voltages depend on the logic family used.

The structure of digital devices is based on the simple concept of repeatedly applying a small number of basic logic circuits. The interconnection of these basic circuits is derived from the problem formulation using purely formal methods. The tools for this are provided by Boolean algebra (George Boole, 1854), which, in the specific case of application to digital circuit technology, is referred to as switching algebra (Claude E. Shannon, 1938).
