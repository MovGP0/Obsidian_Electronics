---
title: Magnetic Tunnel Junction (MTJ) Equations
---
These equations summarize the physical structure implemented in the UMN MTJ SPICE model family. The equations encode these relationships as behavioral voltage/current sources and subcircuits.

## Magnetization dynamics

The free-layer magnetization is represented by the normalized vector:

$$
\mathbf{m} =
\begin{bmatrix}
m_x \\
m_y \\
m_z
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\lVert \mathbf{m} \rVert \approx 1
$$

The model solves a [Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landau%E2%80%93Lifshitz%E2%80%93Gilbert_equation) with spin torque:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{m}}{dt}
=
-\gamma \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{eff}}
-\alpha \gamma \mathbf{m} \times \left(\mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{eff}}\right)
+\mathbf{\tau}_{\mathrm{STT}}
+\mathbf{\tau}_{\mathrm{SHE}}
$$

where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio, $\alpha$ is the Gilbert damping factor, and $\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{eff}}$ combines demagnetizing, anisotropy, external-field, VCMA, and thermal-noise terms depending on the selected model.

## Temperature-dependent material parameters

The LLG solver scales saturation magnetization and spin polarization with temperature:

$$
M_s(T) = M_{s0}\left(1 - \frac{T}{T_\mathrm{curie}}\right)^\beta
$$

$$
P(T) = P_0\left(1 - a_{\mathrm{sp}}T^{1.5}\right)
$$

`Ms0` and `P0` are zero-Kelvin input parameters. `Tmp` is generated by the heat-diffusion subcircuit and is also used by the resistance model.

## MTJ resistance

The resistance subcircuit converts the relative angle between free and pinned layers into a tunnel resistance:

$$
R_{\mathrm{MTJ}}(\theta)
=
\frac{1+\cos\theta}{2}\left(R_P - R_{AP}\right) + R_{AP}
$$

The parallel resistance is calculated from the resistance-area product:

$$
R_P = \frac{RA_0 \cdot 10^{-12}}{l_x l_y}
$$

The model computes a temperature- and voltage-dependent tunnel magnetoresistance and derives $R_{AP}$ from $R_P$ and TMR:

$$
\mathrm{TMR}_0(T)
=
\frac{2P(T)^2}{1-P(T)^2}\cdot 100
$$

$$
R_{AP}
=
R_P\left(1 + \frac{\mathrm{TMR}(V,T)}{100}\right)
$$

## Thermal stability and initial angle

The examples initialize the free layer with a small angle determined by the thermal stability factor. For an in-plane MTJ:

$$
\Delta_{\mathrm{IMA}}
=
\frac{(N_x-N_y)M_s^2 l_x l_y l_z \cdot 10^6}{2 k_B T}
$$

For a crystalline perpendicular MTJ:

$$
\Delta_{\mathrm{cPMA}}
=
\frac{(K_p - 0.5N_zM_s^2)l_x l_y l_z \cdot 10^6}{k_B T}
$$

For an interface perpendicular MTJ:

$$
\Delta_{\mathrm{iPMA}}
=
\frac{2\pi\left(t_c/l_z - N_z/(4\pi)\right)M_s^2 l_x l_y l_z \cdot 10^6}{k_B T}
$$

The initial tilt used in the subcircuits is:

$$
\theta_i = \sin^{-1}\sqrt{\frac{1}{2\Delta}}
$$

## Spin Hall torque

The SHE model adds a spin Hall metal path. The spin Hall angle `SHA` converts charge current in the spin Hall metal into transverse spin current. A compact expression for the damping-like contribution is:

$$
\mathbf{\tau}_{\mathrm{SHE}}
\propto
\frac{\hbar}{2e}
\frac{\theta_{\mathrm{SH}} J_{\mathrm{SHM}}}{M_s t_F}
\mathbf{m}\times(\mathbf{m}\times\boldsymbol{\sigma})
$$

The model also exposes `x_ad`, which adds field-like torque as a fraction of the damping-like torque.

## VCMA effect

The VCMA model modifies interfacial anisotropy with the electric field across the tunnel oxide:

$$
K_i(V)
=
2\pi M_s^2 t_c
-
\xi \frac{V_E}{t_{\mathrm{ox}}}
$$

where $\xi$ is represented by `vcma_coeff`, `VE` is the VCMA voltage, and `tox` is oxide thickness. This term changes the perpendicular anisotropy barrier during the applied pulse, which changes switching probability and operating margin.
